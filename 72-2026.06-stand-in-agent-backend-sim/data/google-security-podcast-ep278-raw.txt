Recorded at Google Cloud Next 2026 within the PwC showcase space, this episode of the Cloud Security Podcast by Google features hosts Tim Peacock and Anton Chuvakin alongside guest Matt Gregson, Principal at PwC US. The conversation centers on the current landscape, realistic capabilities, and future trajectory of "agentic" Security Operations Centers (SOCs). The discussion separates actual industry trends from market hype, analyzing how artificial intelligence transforms threat detection, response architectures, and the fundamental role of human analysts over the next decade.

Detailed Summary and Debrief

Current State of the Art: Co-Pilots vs. True Agentic Pipelines

The discussion initiates with an assessment of how generative AI and agentic frameworks are currently deployed within modern security operations. The consensus indicates that the market is bifurcated into two distinct operating models:

The Co-Pilot Model: This is the most prevalent and mature architecture currently in production. Utilizing large language models (LLMs) such as Gemini integrated via Model Context Protocol (MCP) servers into Security Operations (SecOps) platforms, analysts interact with telemetry through natural language chat interfaces. This capability focuses heavily on querying active detections, parsing specific alerts, and generating high-level trend reports.

The Integrated Agentic Model: True agentic integration—where AI agents actively participate in or orchestrate remediation pipelines—remains largely in the pilot or proof-of-concept phase across most enterprise organizations. Enterprises are currently evaluating where to safely inject agentic decision-making logic into pre-existing deterministic workflows.

The primary friction point preventing fully autonomous operation is organizational comfort and trust. While the long-term industry vision mandates autonomous containment and remediation, security leadership is carefully testing how to establish adequate governance and safety guardrails before granting AI agents full execution rights over infrastructure containment.

The Agentic Overton Window and Value Propositions

The concept of the "Agentic Overton Window" defines the boundary of acceptable, trusted autonomous response behaviors within an enterprise. Historically, deterministic security orchestration, automation, and response (SOAR) workflows successfully established narrow autonomous boxes (e.g., automatically deleting verified phishing emails across corporate mailboxes). The modern security mission requires systematically expanding this window over the coming years, expanding these contained execution boxes into broader, more complex environmental scopes.

When evaluating the concrete business and operational returns of transitioning to an agentic SOC architecture, the benefits scale across multiple corporate levels:

Executive Board and C-Suite Level: The primary outcome is structural risk reduction driven by a dramatic contraction in Mean Time to Respond (MTTR).

Operational Team Level: The integration of autonomous agents addresses the escalating speed of modern threat actors, allowing organizations to match the velocity of highly accelerated attack lifecycles.

The Reality of AI-Enabled Threat Actors

The panel directly addresses the industry debate surrounding "AI-enabled attackers," distinguishing measurable operational realities from marketing sensationalism. The threat is not merely a rhetorical excuse for security failures; it manifests in highly specific downstream technical impacts:

Automated Vulnerability Exploitation: Attackers can utilize specialized LLM pipelines to function as automated "zero-day factories." By significantly lowering the resource, time, and financial barriers required to discover and weaponize novel vulnerabilities, the temporal window between vulnerability disclosure and active, widespread exploitation has effectively collapsed.

Traditional Attack Paths at Machine Speed: Despite utilizing AI to accelerate operations, threat actors continue to rely heavily on well-documented structural attack paths, such as LSASS memory dumping, lateral movement, and standard privilege escalation. Because the fundamental mechanics of the post-exploitation lifecycle remain visible, security teams maintain a distinct opportunity to intercept these paths post-initial access, provided their response infrastructure operates at a corresponding machine speed.

The Evolution of the Human Role: The 2030 SOC

Looking forward to the year 2030—a near-term horizon of only four years—the fundamental role of the human analyst is undergoing a structural shift rather than outright elimination. Due to systemic IT inertia, legacy architecture dependencies (e.g., mainframes, unpatched environments), and basic operational friction, traditional Tier 1 manual triage will not disappear entirely from the global enterprise landscape. However, in modernized organizations, the human workload will shift away from repetitive alert triage toward high-value engineering and oversight disciplines:

Agent Supervision: Human analysts will act as supervisors, reviewing high-speed autonomous actions, validating containment decisions, and auditing agent behavioral strings.

Detection Engineering and Automation Architecture: Human effort will pivot toward constructing, tuning, and maintaining the underlying detection engines, deterministic playbooks, and contextual frameworks that fuel agentic systems.

Threat Landscape Tracking: Continuous alignment of technical defenses with the shifting strategies of global threat actors.

Prerequisites for Agentic Maturity

Organizations cannot bypass foundational security hygiene by deploying an AI agent. True readiness for an agentic SOC architecture requires specific technical prerequisites:

Centralized Data Platforms: A unified, high-scale data engine containing clean, comprehensive telemetry across host systems, networks, and identity providers to allow accurate agent enrichment.

Integrated Core Infrastructure: Centralized platform APIs that grant orchestration layers immediate, programmatic execution control over firewalls, Identity and Access Management (IAM) systems, and Identity Providers (IdPs).

Deterministic SOAR Foundations: High-functioning, well-engineered SOAR playbooks must exist prior to agent introduction. The optimal architecture executes deterministic logic first—handling routine enrichment, threat intelligence lookups, and known variable parsing—before passing the highly curated context to an LLM agent for advanced reasoning. This structure preserves analytical accuracy and prevents catastrophic human context loss if an agent fails to execute correctly.

Architectural Performance: Speed, Economics, and Model Selection

A critical design consideration is the strategic positioning of LLMs within the security engineering pipeline. Passing raw, unfilterable event streams (such as high-volume cloud audit logs) directly into an LLM is architecturally and financially non-viable for standard enterprises.

Deterministic streaming detection and response pipelines operate at speeds significantly faster than token-by-token LLM generation. Therefore, an optimized SOC infrastructure treats AI as a precise tool within a broader multi-tiered economic model:

Tier 1 (Deterministic Automation): Operates at minimal cost (e.g., pennies per execution) and maximal speed. This layer filters out noise and auto-resolves high-confidence alerts via rigid flowcharts without requiring an LLM.

Tier 2 (Agentic LLM Layer): Invoked mid-to-late chain to process complex variables, synthesize threat intel, or summarize dense events.

Tier 3 (Human Execution): Reserved for high-stakes scenarios requiring ultimate manual validation.

Enterprises must conduct rigorous back-testing and implement strict observability frameworks to establish an explicit "alert budget." Operational testing demonstrates that state-of-the-art flagship models are rarely required for everyday security operations; smaller, highly optimized "flash" models from prior release cycles provide exceptional accuracy for event summarization and log analysis at a fraction of the token cost.

Metrics, Governance, and Actionable Advice

As SOC architectures evolve, traditional performance metrics like Mean Time to Detect (MTTD) and Mean Time to Respond (MTTR) remain essential, but must be augmented with agent-specific governance metrics, specifically tracking the number of human touches per case and the overall ratio of autonomous vs. manual containment executions.

To prepare for the practical integration of AI agents as functional "teammates," security leaders are advised to lower the barrier to entry through immediate internal experimentation:

Construct basic AI agents within ring-fenced testing environments.

Connect these experimental agents to existing Model Context Protocol (MCP) servers or internal service APIs.

Evaluate the "art of the possible" by validating how effectively the model queries localized security data before designing complex autonomous workflows.
